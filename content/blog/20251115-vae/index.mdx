## The premise

The initial idea of the VAE is to learn the distribution of $p(x|z)$ in order to be able to generate new samples of the data by sampling $z \sim p(z)$ and then $x \sim p(x|z)$.

It means then that main objective is to maximize the log-likelihood of our data, basically learning the distribution of the data, i.e.

$$\text{maximize} \log{p(x)} = \log{\int{p(x|z) p(z) \delta{z}}}$$

The problem with this equation is that the integral is **intractable**.

## The tractable transformation

We don't have access to $p(z|x)$ so we'll need to approximate it with a neural network that we'll denote $q_{\theta}(z|x)$.
From the initial objective, we'll derive a new a new objective for our neural network that will be effectively be tractable.

Here's, step-by-step, how we obtain the new objective starting from our initial objective:

$$
\begin{align*}
\log{p(x)} &= \log{\int{p(x|z) p(z) \delta{z}}} \\
&= \log{\int{p(x|z) p(z) \dfrac{q_{\theta}(z|x)}{q_{\theta}(z|x)} \delta{z}}} \\
&= \log{\int{q_{\theta}(z|x) \dfrac{p(x|z) p(z)}{q_{\theta}(z|x)} \delta{z}}} \\
&= \log{\mathbb{E}_{z \sim q_{\theta}(z|x)} \left[ \dfrac{p(x|z) p(z)}{q_{\theta}(z|x)} \right]} \quad \text{(becomes an expectation over } q(z|x)) \\
&\geq \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q_{\theta}(z|x)} \left[ \log{ \dfrac{p(x|z) p(z)}{q_{\theta}(z|x)}} \right] \quad \text{(using Jensen's inequality since log is concave)} \\
&= \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q_{\theta}(z|x)} \left[ \log{p(x|z)} + \log{p(z) - \log{q_{\theta}(z|x)}} \right] \\
&= \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{z \sim q_{\theta}(z|x)} \left[ \log{p(x|z)} \right]}_{\text{Reconstruction term}} - \underbrace{\text{KL}(q_{\theta}(z|x) \parallel p(z))}_{\text{Regularization term}} \\
\end{align*}
$$

## The loss function

With this new objective in place, we can derive a loss function for our model based on a dataset $D = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\}$.

This loss can be materialized as the discrete form of our transformed objective:

$$ L = \dfrac{1}{N} \sum_{x_i \in D} [E_{z \sim q(z|x_i)}[\log{p(x_i|z)}] - KL(q(z|x_i) || p(z))] $$

## Reparametrization trick

The only issue with this loss is that we can't backpropagate through the sampling step. We thus need to find a way to have a random sampling step but being able to have a gradient flow between the encoding part of our network and the decoding.

To do this, we can use the reparametrization trick that basically use an external source of randomness.
So, instead of sampling $z$ directly, we'll sample a noise $\epsilon$ from a standard normal distribution $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ and then use the following transformation:

$$ z = g_{\theta}(x, \epsilon) = \mu_{\theta}(x) + \sigma_{\theta}(x) \odot \epsilon $$

where $\mu_{\theta}(x)$ and $\sigma_{\theta}(x)$ are the mean and standard deviation of the latent space, which are also learned by the network.

This way, we can backpropagate all the way through the network. We obtain the following loss function:

$$ L = \dfrac{1}{N} \sum_{x_i \in D} [E_{\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)}[\log{p(x_i|g_{\theta}(x_i, \epsilon))}] - KL(q(z|x_i) || p(z))] $$

# Application: protein sequences

To put this into practice, we train a VAE on protein sequences from the **WW domain** family (Pfam PF00397) [@sinai2017variational], short protein domains of ~35-40 amino acids. Each sequence is one-hot encoded over the 20 standard amino acids and fed through a simple MLP encoder/decoder with a **2D latent space** for easy visualization.
